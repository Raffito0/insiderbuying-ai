# AFTER (Humanized by Ryan Cole)

### Remote Work in 2026: Why I'm Never Going Back to an Office

I've been working remotely for three years now. And honestly? The thought of putting on real pants five days a week again makes me want to cry into my coffee.

But let's talk about why remote work isn't just comfortable — it's actually *better* by almost every measure that matters.

**You actually get stuff done.**

Here's the thing: nobody does eight hours of focused work in an office. You just don't. Between the guy who won't stop talking about his fantasy football team and the three meetings that should've been emails, you're lucky to get four solid hours in. At home, I regularly hit flow states that last two or three hours straight. No shoulder taps. No fire drills. Just work.

The data backs this up too. Buffer's 2026 State of Remote Work report found that 71% of remote workers feel more productive at home. That's not a rounding error.

**The money thing is real.**

I used to spend $340 a month on gas and tolls commuting to an office in the city. Three hundred and forty dollars. Every month. Gone. That's over four grand a year I now spend on, you know, living my life.

Companies save too. No massive lease. No keeping the lights on in a building that's half-empty on Fridays anyway. My last employer cut their real estate costs by 40% after going hybrid. They didn't miss a single revenue target.

**Your brain thanks you.**

Look, I'm not going to pretend remote work is perfect. Loneliness is real. The blur between "work time" and "home time" can get messy. But the tradeoff? I eat actual lunch now instead of sad desk salads. I walk my dog at noon. I was there when my kid took her first steps on a random Tuesday, and that one moment was worth more than every office pizza party combined.

**The planet notices too.**

Fewer cars on the road means fewer emissions. It's that simple. Global Workplace Analytics estimates that if everyone who could work remotely did so even half the time, it'd cut greenhouse gas emissions by 54 million tons per year. That's the equivalent of taking almost 10 million cars off the road.

Will offices disappear completely? Probably not. Some work genuinely needs face-to-face collaboration. But the default has flipped. The question isn't "why remote?" anymore. It's "why not?"

And if your boss still needs everyone in the building for "culture" — maybe the culture just isn't that good.
